Baseline — Broad NPY + sleep/orexin idea

Hypothesis: Broadly explore NPY and orexin/sleep peptides for bipolar mania stabilization.

Score: 4/6

Pass: E1, E3, E5, E6
Fail: E2, E4

Why: NPY is strongly tied to stress resilience and anxiety/stress biology, and orexin is clearly tied to sleep/wake state. But the baseline is too broad: it does not specify whether orexin should be agonized or blocked, and bipolar-specific NPY evidence is weak/indirect.

Decision: Keep as starting scaffold only; mutate toward safer specificity.

Iteration 1 — KEEP

Mutation: Narrow NPY from “general NPY” to NPY stress-resilience with receptor-subtype triage.

Hypothesis: NPY-pathway modulation may stabilize stress reactivity and anxiety/arousal components that contribute to bipolar destabilization, but receptor subtype matters. Prioritize Y1/Y2/Y5 mapping before candidate design.

Score: 5/6

Tests passed: E1, E3, E4, E5, E6
Tests failed: E2

Evidence notes:

  • PMID 15337373 — The NPY system in stress, anxiety and depression. NPY antagonizes behavioral consequences of stress across models; NPY knockout/inactivation increases emotionality.
  • PMID 27913193 — Neuropeptide Y, resilience, and PTSD therapeutics. NPY is a mediator of stress resilience and a potential therapeutic focus in PTSD.
  • OpenTargets confirmed assayable human targets: NPY1R, NPY2R, NPY5R.

Breakpoints discovered:

  • Direct bipolar/mania evidence for NPY is thin.
  • “Increase NPY” is too crude. Receptor-subtype effects may diverge.
  • Sleep stabilization is not yet explicit enough.

Decision: KEEP. It improves mechanistic precision and assayability, but needs sleep/circadian anchor.

Next mutation: Add orexin/hypocretin directionality, especially avoiding orexin agonism.

Iteration 2 — KEEP

Mutation: Add orexin-lowering / orexin-antagonist-informed sleep stabilization as the sleep/circadian module.

Hypothesis: For bipolar mania stabilization, orexin should be treated as a wakefulness/hyperarousal risk pathway. The safer direction is orexin reduction/antagonism or sleep stabilization, not orexin agonism.

Score: 6/6

Tests passed: E1, E2, E3, E4, E5, E6
Tests failed: none

Evidence notes:

  • PMID 20655364 — CSF hypocretin-1 / orexin A studied in mania vs unipolar depression vs controls; establishes direct mania-adjacent orexin evidence.
  • PMID 41127685 — sleep disturbances linked to plasma orexin A levels in bipolar disorder; preliminary but directly relevant.
  • PMID 17950929 — repeated sleep deprivation causes mania-like behaviors in mice; sleep deprivation is a mania-relevant trigger/model.
  • PMID 6873309 — circadian rhythm disturbances in manic-depressive illness.
  • OpenTargets: HCRTR1 and HCRTR2 strongly associated with insomnia/sleep phenotypes.

Breakpoints discovered:

  • Orexin agonism is a hard-risk direction for mania.
  • Existing orexin antagonist drugs are mostly small molecules, not peptides. This is a pathway module, not necessarily a peptide candidate yet.
  • Sedation is not the same as mood stabilization; assay plan must distinguish sleep improvement from anti-manic effect.

Decision: KEEP. This becomes the sleep/circadian safety backbone.

Next mutation: Combine NPY stress-resilience + orexin sleep stabilization into a dual-axis program.

Iteration 3 — KEEP

Mutation: Combine NPY receptor-subtype triage with orexin-antagonist-informed sleep stabilization.

Hypothesis: A dual-axis program — NPY for stress-resilience + orexin modulation for sleep/circadian stabilization — is safer than a single “antidepressant peptide” strategy because it targets two core mania destabilizers: stress arousal and sleep loss.

Iteration 4 — DISCARD / QUARANTINE

Mutation: Add BDNF/TrkB mimetic neuroplasticity as an antidepressant peptide module.

Hypothesis: A BDNF/TrkB mimetic peptide could improve bipolar depression through neuroplasticity.

Score: 3/6

Tests passed: E4, E5, E6
Tests failed: E1, E2, E3

Why it broke:

  • BDNF/TrkB activation is plausibly pro-plasticity and antidepressant-relevant, but for mania stabilization it is too activating unless tightly constrained.
  • It does not directly stabilize sleep.
  • It may drift toward ketamine-like “rapid antidepressant” logic, which is not automatically safe in bipolar mania.
  • It introduces seizure/neuroexcitability and mood-switch concerns requiring heavy guardrails.

Breakpoints discovered:

  • “Antidepressant” is not equal to “safe for bipolar mania.”
  • Neuroplasticity-enhancing pathways should be quarantined until anti-manic/sleep-stable guardrails exist.

Decision: DISCARD / QUARANTINE. Do not include BDNF/TrkB in the first-generation bipolar mania stabilization peptide program.

Next mutation: Convert the kept dual-axis concept into a concrete assay-first program with explicit kill criteria.

Final Result After 5 Iterations

Best current hypothesis

BMS-P1 v0.2: NPY–Orexin Stabilization Axis

A bipolar mania stabilization peptide-pathway research program should start with:

  1. NPY receptor-subtype triage for stress resilience / anti-arousal biology.
  2. Orexin-antagonist-informed sleep/circadian stabilization to avoid sleep-loss-driven mania.
  3. Hard kill criteria against wakefulness, dopamine/glutamate activation, and mania-like behavior.

Best candidate direction

Not a final peptide yet. The best next step is a target-selection and assay program:

  • NPY1R vs NPY2R vs NPY5R functional mapping
  • HCRTR1/HCRTR2 modulation as a sleep-stability comparator
  • sleep-deprivation mania model / circadian rhythm readouts
  • dopamine/glutamate activation exclusion

Quarantined direction

BDNF/TrkB mimetic peptides: interesting for depression biology, but fail first-pass mania stabilization criteria unless heavily constrained.

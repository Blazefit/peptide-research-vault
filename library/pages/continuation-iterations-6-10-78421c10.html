BMS-P1 Continuation — Iterations 6–10

Research-only boundary: This is target-selection and assay-planning work for severe bipolar mania stabilization. It is not treatment advice, dosing guidance, compounding guidance, or a self-experimentation plan.

Prior State

Previous best scaffold:

BMS-P1 v0.2 — NPY–Orexin Stabilization Axis

Core thesis:

  1. NPY pathway for stress-resilience / anti-arousal biology.
  2. Orexin/hypocretin pathway for sleep/circadian stabilization.
  3. Hard kill criteria: no insomnia increase, no dopamine/glutamate activation, no mania-like stimulation, no unexamined serotonergic/seizure/suicidality risk.
  4. BDNF/TrkB remains quarantined.

Iteration 6 — KEEP

Mutation

Refine the NPY module from “NPY subtype triage” to Y1-biased stress-resilience lead hypothesis.

Hypothesis

A Y1-biased NPY pathway is the most plausible peptide-first stress-resilience module because the NPY literature repeatedly points to central anti-stress/anxiolytic effects, and Y1 is historically implicated in anti-stress signaling.

Score

6/7

Eval Result Notes
E1 anti-manic Pass Stress-arousal dampening is plausibly stabilizing
E2 sleep-safe Pass Not wake-promoting; may indirectly support sleep by reducing arousal
E3 non-activating Pass Not primarily DA/glutamate activating
E4 evidence Pass NPY stress/anxiety/depression evidence strong; bipolar-specific evidence weak
E5 assayable Pass NPY1R receptor assays, stress behavior models
E6 delivery Fail CNS peptide delivery is still unresolved
E7 safety Pass Mania activation risk appears lower than BDNF/orexin agonism, but still needs behavioral screen

Evidence notes

  • PMID 15337373 — NPY antagonizes behavioral consequences of stress and shows potent anti-stress effects across models.
  • PMID 12013027 — NPY Y1 receptors implicated in anti-stress effects.
  • PMID 21995655 — NPY has central functions in mood/anxiety disorders; Y1/Y2 best understood.
  • ChEMBL confirms assayable NPY1R targets and ligands.

Breakpoints

  • The Y1 direction is still adjacent evidence, not bipolar-mania-specific evidence.
  • CNS delivery is the biggest unresolved failure.
  • NPY can influence feeding, blood pressure, circadian function, and memory; these cannot be ignored.

Decision

KEEP as primary peptide lead axis.

New label: BMS-P1A — Y1-biased NPY stress-resilience module.

Iteration 7 — SECONDARY / CAUTION

Mutation

Test a Y2-modulation strategy as an alternative or adjunct.

Hypothesis

Y2 modulation may influence anxiety/stress biology, possibly through autoreceptor effects on NPY release, but the direction is more complex than Y1.

Score

5/7

Eval Result Notes
E1 anti-manic Pass Could reduce stress/arousal depending direction
E2 sleep-safe Unclear/fail Directional sleep effects not sufficiently constrained
E3 non-activating Pass Not obviously stimulant-like
E4 evidence Pass NPY receptor literature supports Y2 relevance
E5 assayable Pass NPY2R assays and ligands exist
E6 delivery Fail Same CNS delivery problem
E7 safety Pass No immediate hard-stop, but subtype complexity is a caution

Evidence notes

  • PMID 21995655 — Y1 and Y2 are best-understood subtypes in mood/anxiety context.
  • PMID 12359508 — genetically modified animals reveal diverse NPY functions and receptor-specific complexity.
  • PMID 28824541 — Y1, Y2, and Y5 relevant to affective disorders and sleep regulation.

Breakpoints

  • Y2 is biologically complex; agonism vs antagonism may have opposite effects depending circuit.
  • Could alter feeding/metabolic behavior, stress response, and possibly seizure threshold.
  • Not a clean first lead compared with Y1.

Decision

Do not discard, but demote to secondary.

New label: BMS-P1B — Y2 subtype exploration / secondary module.

Iteration 8 — DISCARD AS PEPTIDE LEAD, KEEP AS COMPARATOR

Mutation

Attempt to convert the orexin module into a peptide antagonist lead.

Hypothesis

A peptide antagonist or peptide-derived blocker of HCRTR1/HCRTR2 could directly suppress wakefulness/hyperarousal and stabilize sleep in mania-risk states.

Score

4/7

Eval Result Notes
E1 anti-manic Pass Orexin suppression is directionally anti-wakefulness
E2 sleep-safe Pass if antagonist Direction must remain antagonist/suppression only
E3 non-activating Pass Antagonism is not stimulant-like
E4 evidence Pass Orexin/sleep/bipolar evidence exists
E5 assayable Pass HCRTR1/HCRTR2 assays exist
E6 delivery Fail Peptide antagonist CNS access likely poor
E7 safety Fail Too easy to accidentally drift into orexin agonism or broad arousal-system disruption

Evidence notes

  • PMID 20655364 — hypocretin/orexin A studied directly in mania.
  • PMID 41127685 — orexin A linked to sleep disturbances in bipolar disorder.
  • PMID 16036174 — orexin has non-sleep effects including neuroendocrine/autonomic/stress functions.
  • ChEMBL shows rich HCRTR1/HCRTR2 pharmacology, but mostly small-molecule ligand space.

Breakpoints

  • Existing clinically useful orexin antagonism is small-molecule dominated, not peptide dominated.
  • Orexin peptides themselves are wake-promoting; a peptide program here risks wrong-direction biology.
  • For a peptide-development program, orexin is better as an assay/safety comparator, not first peptide candidate.

Decision

DISCARD as peptide lead. KEEP as comparator/safety backbone.

New label: BMS-C1 — DORA-informed orexin comparator module.

Iteration 9 — KEEP

Mutation

Add a delivery-first constraint: any NPY peptide lead must be evaluated through intranasal/CNS-targeted feasibility before sequence optimization.

Hypothesis

Because NPY is a CNS peptide and systemic delivery is unlikely to cleanly reach the right brain circuits, the program should not optimize peptide sequence until it has a credible brain-delivery route. Intranasal delivery is the most plausible research route to evaluate, not a recommendation for use.

Score

7/7

Eval Result Notes
E1 anti-manic Pass Keeps stress-arousal mechanism
E2 sleep-safe Pass Delivery constraint does not add wakefulness risk
E3 non-activating Pass No DA/glutamate activation added
E4 evidence Pass Intranasal neuropeptide/CNS delivery literature exists
E5 assayable Pass Brain exposure, receptor activity, behavior readouts
E6 delivery Pass Delivery is now explicit and testable
E7 safety Pass Still research-only with hard-stop flags

Evidence notes

  • PMID 41898218 — intranasal drug delivery can bypass BBB barriers for brain-targeted therapies, with bioethical caveats.
  • PMID 35250569 — intranasal delivery of NPY1 agonists in rats provides proof-of-concept that NPY receptor peptide strategies can be studied via intranasal CNS delivery.
  • PMID 21995655 — NPY has central functions in mood/anxiety disorders.

Breakpoints

  • Intranasal delivery has huge translational variability.
  • Human anatomy, deposition, formulation stability, and local tolerability matter.
  • CNS exposure must be measured, not assumed.

Decision

KEEP. This resolves the biggest failure from Iterations 6–7.

New label: BMS-P1A-IN — intranasal/CNS-delivery-gated Y1-biased NPY module.

Iteration 10 — KEEP AS CURRENT LEAD PROGRAM

Mutation

Convert the program into a two-track lead architecture:

  1. Peptide lead: Y1-biased NPY stress-resilience module, delivery-gated.
  2. Non-peptide comparator: DORA / orexin antagonist-informed sleep-stabilization benchmark.

Hypothesis

The safest near-term architecture is not “one peptide to do everything.” It is a peptide stress-resilience lead tested against a sleep/circadian safety comparator so that mania-stabilization readouts cannot be confused with generic sedation or antidepressant activation.

BMS-P1 v0.3 Final Program Definition

Name

BMS-P1 v0.3 — Delivery-Gated Y1-NPY Stress Resilience + Orexin Comparator Stabilization Program

One-line thesis

A mania-stabilization peptide program should start with a Y1-biased NPY stress-resilience peptide concept gated by CNS delivery and anti-activation screens, while using orexin antagonist-informed sleep stabilization as a comparator and safety benchmark.

Next critical tests

  1. Y1 vs Y2 receptor directionality map - Determine whether desired behavioral effects align with Y1 agonism, Y2 antagonism, or another subtype pattern.

  2. Delivery feasibility gate - Intranasal/CNS exposure model, peptide stability, local tolerability, distribution.

  3. Sleep-deprivation mania-like model - Must reduce destabilization without sedation-only artifact.

  4. Orexin comparator benchmark - Use HCRTR1/HCRTR2 antagonist-informed comparator to define sleep-stability readouts.

  5. DA/glutamate hard-kill screen - Any stimulant-like activation kills candidate.

  6. Safety hard-stop screen - Seizure threshold, suicidality proxy, serotonergic interaction, behavioral activation, cardiovascular/autonomic effects.

Important limitations

  • This is not a treatment protocol.
  • The NPY evidence is strong for stress/anxiety/resilience, but not yet strong directly for bipolar mania.
  • Orexin is better as a comparator/safety readout than a peptide lead.
  • Intranasal peptide delivery is plausible for research, but variable and nontrivial.
  • Any candidate must survive anti-mania, sleep-stability, and non-activation screens before advancing.

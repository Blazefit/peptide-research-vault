Slide 1 / 16 · Title

Peptides 101

Signals, Strategy & Safety

Subtitle: What peptides are, why GLP-1s changed the world, and how to think about this space without getting reckless.

Speaker notes

Tonight is not a “here’s what you should take” talk. It is a map. Peptides are a huge category: some are FDA-approved drugs, some are promising research tools, and some are internet hype with very thin evidence.

Slide 2 / 16 · The Big Idea

A peptide is a message

  • Peptides are short chains of amino acids.
  • The body uses them as signals.
  • Insulin, GLP-1, oxytocin, and growth-hormone signals are all peptide-related biology.
  • Modern peptide therapy often means taking a synthetic version of a signal the body already understands.

Visual idea: Body as a “message network”: gut → brain → pancreas → muscle → fat cells.

Speaker notes

The simplest way to understand peptides is that they are messages. Hunger is a message. Fullness is a message. Repair is a message. Inflammation is a message. The question is: can we adjust those signals safely and intelligently?

Slide 3 / 16 · Why Peptides Are Exploding Now

The GLP-1 revolution changed the conversation

For decades, weight loss advice was mostly:

  1. Eat less
  2. Move more
  3. Try harder

Those still matter. But GLP-1 drugs showed that appetite, cravings, blood sugar, and body weight are signal-driven, not just willpower-driven.

Key line: If hunger is a signal, the signal can be changed.

Speaker notes

This is why people are paying attention. Semaglutide and tirzepatide proved that changing a biological signal can change behavior and outcomes. That opened the door for people to ask what other signals can be supported: recovery, inflammation, mitochondrial output, libido, focus, tissue repair.

This is the slide I want everyone to remember. “Peptide” does not automatically mean safe, effective, or experimental. It depends which one we are talking about and what evidence exists.

Slide 5 / 16 · Group 1: Fat-Loss & Metabolic Peptides

Fat loss is not one pathway

AOD-9604 — GH fragment designed around lipolysis; human data exists but modest effect.

5-Amino-1MQ — technically not a peptide; targets NNMT/metabolic signaling.

Tesofensine — not a peptide; brain/craving pathway, originally studied outside obesity.

Cagrilintide / CagriSema — amylin/fullness pathway; stronger when paired with GLP-1.

Melanotan II — multi-pathway; tanning/libido/appetite effects, not a clean fat-loss tool.

Speaker notes

The public lumps all of this together as “fat-burning peptides,” but they are not the same. Some affect appetite. Some affect energy handling. Some affect multiple receptors and bring more side-effect complexity.

Slide 6 / 16 · Group 2: GLP-1s & Next-Gen Incretins

The most proven peptide-adjacent weight-loss category

  • Semaglutide — GLP-1; strong evidence; FDA-approved for obesity as Wegovy.
  • Tirzepatide — GLP-1 + GIP; stronger average weight loss; FDA-approved as Zepbound/Mounjaro depending indication.
  • Retatrutide — GLP-1 + GIP + glucagon; extremely promising, not FDA-approved yet.
  • Survodutide / Mazdutide / Cotadutide — next-gen incretin designs, often targeting fatty liver/metabolic disease subgroups.

Clinic framing: These require labs, contraindication screening, nutrition strategy, strength training, and long-term planning.

Slide 7 / 16 · Group 3: Growth Hormone Signaling

Recovery, sleep, body composition — but not magic

  • CJC-1295 / Sermorelin — GHRH-style signal: tells pituitary to release GH.
  • Ipamorelin — ghrelin-receptor signal: amplifies GH pulses with less cortisol/prolactin activity than older GHRPs.
  • Tesamorelin — stronger GHRH analog; FDA-approved for HIV-associated visceral fat.
  • GHRP-2 / GHRP-6 / Hexarelin — older, stronger, but more side-effect baggage.

Key caution: GH-axis conversations should include IGF-1, glucose/A1c, sleep apnea risk, edema, carpal-tunnel symptoms, cancer history, and goals.

Slide 8 / 16 · Group 4: Mitochondrial & Energy Peptides

Energy starts inside the cell

  • MOTS-c — mitochondrial-derived peptide; often discussed as an AMPK/exercise-mimetic signal.
  • SS-31 / Elamipretide — mitochondrial membrane/cardiolipin support; stronger clinical development than most “research peptides.”
  • Humanin — mitochondrial-derived peptide linked to aging/metabolic resilience research.
  • SLU-PP-332 — not a peptide; exercise-mimetic research compound, very early.
Speaker notes

This category is exciting but easy to overhype. The question is not “Can this replace exercise?” It is “What cellular bottleneck are we actually trying to support?”

Slide 9 / 16 · Group 5: Repair, Inflammation & Tissue Support

The recovery stack is where hype gets loud

  • BPC-157 — strong preclinical repair story; limited human clinical proof.
  • TB-500 / Thymosin β4 fragments — cell migration, actin, tissue-repair discussion; limited human evidence.
  • GHK-Cu — copper peptide; skin/hair/wound-healing signal with broader cosmetic use.
  • KPV / Thymosin Alpha-1 — immune/inflammation pathways.
  • Cartalax / bioregulators — cartilage/bone claims, but evidence remains thin.

Clinic framing: Tissue repair still starts with diagnosis, imaging when needed, rehab loading, protein, sleep, micronutrients, and inflammation control.

Slide 10 / 16 · Group 6: Brain, Cravings & Behavior

The brain is part of metabolism

  • Oxytocin — fullness/emotional-eating research angle.
  • PT-141 / Bremelanotide — FDA-approved for HSDD; melanocortin/reward-pathway overlap.
  • Kisspeptin-10 — reproductive hormone signaling tied to metabolic state.
  • Semax / Selank — Russian nootropic/anxiolytic peptides; limited Western clinical validation.

Key line: Fat loss is not only calories; it is appetite, reward, stress, sleep, hormones, and environment.

Slide 11 / 16 · How to Think About Stacking

The biggest mistake: stacking everything at once

Bad approach:

  • 5–8 compounds at once
  • No baseline labs
  • No symptom tracking
  • No clear goal
  • No way to know what worked or what caused side effects

Better approach:

  1. Define the goal.
  2. Get baseline labs and red-flag screening.
  3. Fix basics first: protein, training, sleep, alcohol, steps.
  4. Add one intervention at a time.
  5. Track outcomes and side effects.
Slide 12 / 16 · Labs-First Peptide Strategy

Before the peptide, ask: what problem are we solving?

Useful baseline areas:

  • CBC / CMP
  • A1c, fasting glucose, fasting insulin
  • Lipids, ApoB if available
  • Thyroid markers
  • Sex hormones when relevant
  • IGF-1 for GH-axis conversations
  • Inflammatory markers where appropriate
  • Blood pressure, waist, body composition, sleep quality
Speaker notes

Peptides are not a replacement for assessment. If someone is tired, overweight, inflamed, or injured, we should not guess. We should identify the bottleneck.

Slide 13 / 16 · Safety & Red Flags

Who should slow down or get medical oversight?

  • Personal/family history of medullary thyroid cancer or MEN2 for GLP-1 class discussions
  • Pancreatitis/gallbladder history
  • Active cancer or unexplained masses
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding
  • Uncontrolled diabetes or hypoglycemia risk
  • Severe GI symptoms
  • Sleep apnea / edema / carpal tunnel in GH-axis discussions
  • Autoimmune complexity for immune-modulating peptides
  • Any peptide from a questionable source with no COA / purity testing

Key line: The goal is not “more compounds.” The goal is better decisions.

Slide 15 / 16 · What Peptides Won’t Do

Peptides are accelerators, not foundations

They will not replace:

  • Protein intake
  • Strength training
  • Sleep
  • Sunlight / circadian rhythm
  • Rehab progression
  • Alcohol reduction
  • Bloodwork and medical screening
  • Consistency
Speaker notes

This is where we keep the room grounded. If someone sleeps four hours, eats poorly, drinks too much, and never trains, no vial fixes that. Peptides may help the right person at the right time, but they do not replace foundations.

Slide 16 / 16 · Closing Framework

The CrossFit Blaze Peptide Framework

  1. Goal — What are we trying to change?
  2. Evidence — Is this FDA-approved, human-trial, preclinical, or anecdotal?
  3. Labs — What markers tell us if it makes sense?
  4. Safety — Who should not touch this without a doctor?
  5. Tracking — How will we know if it helped?
  6. Basics — Are training, nutrition, sleep, and rehab already handled?

Final line: Peptides are not magic. They are signals. Better signal, better strategy — when used carefully.
